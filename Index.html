Background
What We're Studying

Physics connects the invisible forces of our universe to the everyday world. This module explores four interconnected pillars of the AP Physics syllabus.

🌍

Why Physics Matters

Physics is the language the universe speaks. From the spark of lightning to the warmth of sunlight, every phenomenon traces back to fundamental forces and energy. Understanding electricity, magnetism, heat, and measurement equips us to explain — and engineer — the world around us. This module bridges abstract equations with real, tangible experiences in your daily life.

⚡

Static Electricity

Born from unbalanced charges, static electricity makes hair stand up, balloons cling to walls, and lightning tear through the sky. Atoms — with protons, neutrons, and electrons — are the source of all electric phenomena.

🧲

Magnetism

Magnetism arises from moving charges within atoms. When domains in iron or cobalt align, a magnet is born. Link electricity and magnetism, and you get electromagnetism — the force behind motors, speakers, and MRI machines.

📏

Measurement

Science demands precision. The International System of Units (SI) gives researchers a shared language — meters, kilograms, Kelvin — to compare and replicate results across the globe.

🌡️

Thermal Physics

Heat, temperature, and energy flow explain why oceans moderate climate, why sweat cools us, and how fridges work. Thermal physics bridges particle-level motion to the macroscopic world we experience every day.

⚡ Electric Fields & Lightning

In storm clouds, colliding ice particles transfer charge, building enormous electric fields. When strong enough — lightning strikes. Coulomb's Law at planetary scale.

F = k · q₁q₂ / r²

⚛️ Inside an Atom

  • Protons (+1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C) & neutrons in nucleus
  • Electrons (−1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C) orbit the nucleus
  • Neutral atom: equal protons & electrons
  • Ion: atom with a net charge
  • Conductors let charge flow; insulators resist

🔄 Methods of Charging

  • Friction: electrons transfer when rubbed
  • Induction: charge rearranges without contact
  • Conduction: charge transfers on direct contact
  • Earthing allows safe discharge to ground
  • Polarization: charge separates, object stays neutral

🌐 Van de Graaff Generator

Charges accumulate on a large metal sphere. Touch it and charge spreads over your body — each hair repels the others and stands up! Must stand on an insulating mat.

E = kQs/r² or E = F/Qt

🧲 Magnetic Field Lines

Iron filings reveal the invisible magnetic field — densest near the poles. Field lines always run North → South outside the magnet.

  • Strongest at the poles
  • Like poles repel; unlike poles attract
  • U-shaped magnet creates a uniform field

💡 Electromagnetism & Solenoids

Pass current through a coil and it behaves like a bar magnet. Reverse the current, reverse the field. Increase current, strengthen the field.

  • Current → circular field around wire
  • Solenoid acts like a bar magnet
  • Electromagnets: switchable & adjustable

🧭 Types of Magnets

  • Permanent: keeps magnetism long-term (steel)
  • Temporary: magnetic only near a field (soft iron)
  • Magnetize: stroke, solenoid, or proximity
  • Demagnetize: heat, drop, or alternating field
  • Magnetic materials: iron, steel, nickel, cobalt

📐 Magnetic Domains

Deep inside magnetic materials, groups of atoms called domains share the same magnetic orientation. When enough align — the object becomes a magnet.

  • Unaligned domains → no overall magnetism
  • Aligned domains → permanent magnet
  • Heat disrupts alignment → demagnetizes

📏 Length Measurement

SI unit: meter (m). From nanometers (atoms) to kilometers (cities), each prefix scales by powers of 10.

  • 1 km = 1,000 m — between cities
  • 1 cm = 0.01 m — everyday objects
  • 1 mm = 0.001 m — fine details
  • 1 µm = 10⁻⁶ m — cells & biology
  • 1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m — atoms & nanotech

🌡️ Temperature Scales

Temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles. Three main scales are used worldwide.

  • Celsius (°C): weather & daily use
  • Fahrenheit (°F): used in the USA
  • Kelvin (K): scientific calculations
K = °C + 273

⚖️ Mass

Mass is the amount of matter in an object. SI unit: kilogram (kg). Mass is constant regardless of gravity — unlike weight.

  • 1 g = 0.001 kg — light objects
  • 1 mg = 10⁻⁶ kg — medicines
  • 1 tonne = 1,000 kg — heavy loads

🔥 Specific Heat Capacity

Energy needed to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1°C. High SHC = heats slowly; low SHC = heats fast.

  • Water: 4200 J/kg°C — stabilizes Earth's climate
  • Aluminium: 900 J/kg°C — heats up quickly
  • Used in car radiators & industrial cooling
  • Body's water helps regulate temperature
Q = mcΔT

❄️ Specific Latent Heat

During a phase change, temperature stays constant — all energy breaks molecular bonds instead.

  • Fusion (Lf): solid → liquid
  • Vaporization (Lv): liquid → gas
  • Sweating uses Lv to cool the body
  • Fridge/AC use evaporation cycles
Q = mL

🌊 Heat Transfer

  • Conduction: direct contact (metals)
  • Convection: fluid movement (boiling water)
  • Radiation: electromagnetic waves (sunlight)
PV = nRT (Ideal Gas)

🌡️ Temperature & Heat

  • Temperature = average kinetic energy of particles
  • Higher temp → particles move faster
  • Heat flows from hot → cold
  • Thermal equilibrium: temperatures equalise
  • Unit of heat: Joule (J)
Quick Reference
Key Formulas ✦

Every equation you need, beautifully at a glance.

⚡

Coulomb's Law

F = kq₁q₂/r²

k = 9×10⁹ N·m²/C²
q = charge (C) · r = distance (m)

🌐

Electric Field

E = kQs/r²

Also: E = F/Qt
Unit: N/C or V/m

🔥

Specific Heat Capacity

Q = mcΔT

m = mass (kg) · c = SHC (J/kg°C)
ΔT = temperature change

❄️

Specific Latent Heat

Q = mL

L = latent heat (J/kg)
No temp change during phase change

🌡️

Temperature Conversion

K = °C + 273

°F = (9/5 × °C) + 32
Kelvin is always ≥ 0

💨

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

P = pressure · V = volume
n = moles · R = 8.314 J/mol·K

Exercise Questions 💕

Click each question to reveal the full worked solution. Try solving it yourself first!

Q1 — Aluminum block: 2 kg, heated 25°C → 35°C, c = 900 J/kg°C
SHC▼
Formula: Q = mcΔT · ΔT = 35 − 25 = 10°C
Q = 2 × 900 × 10
✅ Q = 18,000 J
Q2 — 500 g water heated 20°C → 60°C, c = 4200 J/kg°C
SHC▼
Convert: 500 g = 0.5 kg · ΔT = 40°C
Q = 0.5 × 4200 × 40
✅ Q = 84,000 J
Q3 — Melt 3 kg of ice at 0°C (Lf = 334,000 J/kg)
Latent Heat▼
Q = mL = 3 × 334,000
✅ Q = 1,002,000 J = 1.002 × 10⁶ J
Q4 — 2 kg water → steam at 100°C (Lv = 2,260,000 J/kg)
Latent Heat▼
Q = mL = 2 × 2,260,000
✅ Q = 4,520,000 J = 4.52 × 10⁶ J
Q5 — Melt 1 kg ice at 0°C then heat water to 20°C (combined)
Combined▼
Step 1 – Melt ice: Q₁ = 1 × 334,000 = 334,000 J
Step 2 – Heat 0→20°C: Q₂ = 1 × 4200 × 20 = 84,000 J
Step 3 – Total: Q = Q₁ + Q₂
✅ Q_total = 418,000 J
Q6 — Why does water heat up slower than metal?
Concept▼
✅ Water has a very high SHC (4200 J/kg°C) vs metals (~400–900). It needs far more energy to raise its temperature by 1°C.
Q7 — Why does sweating cool the body?
Concept▼
✅ Sweat evaporation absorbs large heat from skin due to water's high Specific Latent Heat of Vaporization, carrying thermal energy away and cooling the body.
